Our Philosophy • Ready for Real Life

Prepared to Know.
Ready to Live Well.

Ready for Real Life Instruction and Education is built on one conviction: education should prepare students not only to know, but to live well.

Where It Comes From

This philosophy did not start in a classroom. It started on patrol.

Co-founder Mike Terry spent nearly two decades in law enforcement and public service, including school resource officer work and youth instruction. Across thousands of calls, one pattern kept repeating: most crises did not begin with a lack of academic knowledge. They began with unmanaged emotion, a communication breakdown, an impulsive decision, or a refusal to take accountability.

Mike and Mekenzi Terry built Ready for Real Life on the belief that many of those outcomes can change when students learn decision-making, emotional regulation, respectful communication, accountability, and repair before adulthood, not after. Law enforcement responds after the bad decision. Education gets to work before it.

“If the world students will enter is real, then preparation for that world belongs in the curriculum.”
Mike Terry • Co-founder, Ready for Real Life
The Core Statement
Education should develop judgment, character, communication, emotional regulation, accountability, and the ability to transfer learning into real pressure moments. Academic learning and life readiness are not competing goals. They are the same job, done completely.
Three Questions Every Curriculum Answers

What is real, how do students learn, and what is worth teaching on purpose

What is real for students?

Real life includes pressure, conflict, emotion, digital permanence, family, work, and consequences. A curriculum that treats these as distractions from real learning has it backwards. This curriculum treats them as legitimate educational content.

How do students learn these skills?

Not by hearing them described once. Skills are named and taught explicitly, practiced in realistic scenarios, processed through reflection, and assessed in unfamiliar situations. The design standard is transfer, not coverage.

What is worth teaching on purpose?

Every classroom teaches values through what it rewards, tolerates, and ignores. This curriculum teaches respect, dignity, self-control, accountability, and repair intentionally, so they become observable and practiced rather than assumed.

“Foundational knowledge and skills must be taught directly, and hoping students absorb them is not a method.”
Mike Terry • Co-founder, Ready for Real Life
